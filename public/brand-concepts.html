Five Logo Concepts

Reduced to the essential gesture. Each mark is a single idea — maximum three elements, legible at 16px.

Concepts

01 The Stem Two strokes
Good Measure
measure what matters
large · small · dark

Visual Structure

A vertical line. One horizontal tick extending right from it, placed at a position that reads as "partway up" — not centered, not at the top. Two strokes, one weight, one accent. That is the entire mark.

Symbolic Meaning

The mark you make on a measuring stick. A tally. A calibration point. The tick is placed at roughly 65% — you have a goal, and you're working toward it. The stem is the range; the tick is where you are.

Typography

Bricolage Grotesque 700 for the wordmark. DM Mono uppercase tagline. The mark's verticality echoes the letterforms' stems — it doesn't compete, it rhymes.

Why it resonates: Cooks mark their measuring cups. Researchers mark their baselines. This is that gesture. Radically simple, infinitely scalable. Works at 16px as a favicon because it's just two lines.
02 The Gauge Two-tone dial arc

Visual Structure

A 240° dial arc with the gap at the bottom — the classic gauge/speedometer shape. Two strokes, same path: sage green for the measured portion (65%), stone for the remaining goal (35%). The gap is wide enough to be obviously intentional. Monochrome version collapses to a single arc.

Symbolic Meaning

A gauge reads a value against a range. The two-tone arc says: here is your total range, here is where you are within it. The gap at the bottom is stable, grounded — like a speedometer or a dial thermometer, it's a tool you trust. The sage portion is always the measured amount; the stone portion is what remains.

Typography

The gauge is symmetric on its vertical axis — the wordmark locks neatly to its right at optical center. The arc's open bottom creates natural visual space for the tagline to sit beneath if needed. Bricolage Grotesque 700 for "Good Measure."

Why it resonates: A gauge is an instrument of precision — it shows a reading, not a state. The two-tone split makes the mark instantly meaningful without explanation: you're measuring something against a goal. The 240° arc is wide enough to read clearly at 16px favicon size, and the gap at the bottom is too deliberate to be mistaken for a broken circle.

Visual Structure

"GOOD" in Bricolage 800, extra-bold, tight tracking. A full-width 2px sage rule. "MEASURE" in Bricolage 300, wide tracking. Three elements: one word, one line, one word. No icon ever.

Symbolic Meaning

A typographer's "rule" is a horizontal line used as a measurement and divider. To "rule" something is also to govern it. The mark makes three assertions at once: GOOD (the claim) / — (the standard) / MEASURE (the method). A complete thought in pure type.

Typography

The weight contrast — 800 vs. 300 — carries the entire design. GOOD is the heavy assertion; MEASURE is the precise, considered qualifier. This contrast is the brand personality in typographic form.

Why it resonates: No icon means no competition on the logomark's conceptual territory. Editorial wordmarks are extremely rare in this category — every nutrition app has an icon. This positions Good Measure as something closer to a cookbook or reference tool. It will not look dated in 10 years.
04 The Lines Two strokes, one accent
Good Measure
measure what matters
large · small · dark

Visual Structure

Two horizontal lines, vertically stacked. The top line is full-width (the goal, the standard). The bottom line is 65% width in sage green (the current measurement). Two strokes, aligned left. That's all.

Symbolic Meaning

A before and after. A ruler and a reading. The gap between the two lines' endpoints is where meaning lives — that's the delta between where you are and where you're going. Horizontal marks on a scale, graduated lines on a measuring vessel, the two lines in a comparison chart.

Typography

The horizontal mark pairs naturally with the wordmark set beneath it. The two-line structure also echoes the two-line lock-up from Concept 03 — these can coexist as a system (icon variant vs. wordmark variant of the same brand).

Why it resonates: Two lines is the simplest possible way to show "comparison" and "measurement" without any literal imagery. It's abstract enough to be timeless, specific enough to mean something. The left-aligned gap on the bottom line creates a visual tension that pulls the eye — you want that gap to close.

Visual Structure

A single diagonal line. Used two ways: open (just the stroke, paired with wordmark) and contained (the stroke inside a rounded square for app icon and favicon use). No other marks. The contained variant uses a filled background — ink, sage, or dark.

Symbolic Meaning

The slash is the universal symbol of division, ratio, and "per" — as in grams per serving, kcal per day, protein/carb ratio. It's also the mark that separates a numerator from a denominator. In cooking and nutrition, everything meaningful is a ratio.

Typography

The open slash is used as a standing mark beside the wordmark. The contained slash lives as the app icon and badge — distinct from the wordmark context. Both are one stroke, which means the mark is always in harmony with whatever weight of type it sits near.

Why it resonates: The most reduced possible mark. One line cannot be confused with any other brand. The slash has inherent energy — it's a forward-leaning, purposeful gesture. The contained version is a world-class app icon: dark background, single stroke, instantly legible at any size. The app icon / open mark duality gives maximum flexibility.
