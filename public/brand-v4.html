§ Brand Exploration · v4

Four standalone marks. Each works at favicon size.

Last round's brackets treated the wordmark as the mark — wrong. The mark has to stand alone. This round, every option lives inside a square, reads as an object, and pairs with the wordmark rather than wrapping it.

T1 and T4 read clearly as measurement. T2 and T3 are more abstract — they imply precision without literally depicting it. Looking at these together should tell you which instinct is yours.

All marks are drawn in a 32×32 grid. The large renderings are the same geometry scaled up — what you see at favicon size is the mark, not a simplification of it.

T1 · Paired ticks, framed
Literal · measurement
The interval, contained

Two ticks. One frame. One unit.

Reads as: measurement

The v06 concept, made standalone. Two ticks held inside a square frame — the whole mark becomes one cell on a ruler. The tick spacing inside the square is proportioned so the gap is 2× a tick's width, creating a clear visual rhythm. Works at any scale from 120px display down to 16×16 favicon.

T2 · Grid plate
Abstract · precision
The mark is the measurement surface

A small plate, ruled in four.

Reads as: precision, structure, graph paper

A square divided into four equal quadrants by a horizontal and a vertical rule. Reads as graph paper, a data plate, a reference grid — the surface on which things are measured rather than the measurement itself. Extremely balanced. The most "engineered" of the four.

T3 · Registration mark
Abstract · print
From the printmaker's vocabulary

A registration mark. A plus in a circle.

Reads as: alignment, precision, printing

The mark printers use to align color plates — a cross inside a circle, absolute center on a page. Reads as precision without being a ruler. Connects to your printmaking instincts (risograph, printed goods, stationery) and to the editorial feel of the rest of the brand. Historically earned shape; nothing invented.

T4 · Tally
Literal · measurement
Measurement's oldest form

Four tick marks crossed by a fifth. One count of five.

Reads as: counting, recording, measurement

The tally mark — the way humans have counted for longer than writing has existed. Four vertical ticks with a diagonal slash crossing them: one bundle of five. It's actively measuring — not pointing at measurement, not symbolizing it. The mark is a record. Centered in a square for standalone use.

§ My read
Where I'd put my money

T1 (paired ticks, framed) and T3 (registration mark) are the two real contenders. Let me be honest about each.

T1 is the rigorous answer. It takes the paired-ticks concept you liked, compacts it into a standalone square, and becomes a self-sufficient object. The frame turns the mark into a unit — one cell on a ruler. The favicon is strong. The wordmark pairing is balanced. The only risk: it still has a family resemblance to the bracketed idea you just rejected, and you might feel the same "something's off" reaction.

T3 is the emotional answer. A registration mark is a historically earned shape with real precision-industry credibility. It connects to your background — printmaking, risograph, the analog work you're moving toward — in a way the other marks don't. It's also the most visually satisfying: a circle with a cross is among the oldest, cleanest, most universally readable marks in graphic design. The risk: it's familiar. A thousand small studios use a variant of it. You'd be joining a lineage, not starting one.

T2 (grid plate) is beautiful and too quiet. A 4-quadrant grid at favicon size reads as a loading spinner or a Microsoft tile. Skip.

T4 (tally) is specific and opinionated — a real swing. I like it more than I expected to when I started drawing it. But it has a problem: at 16px favicon size it's almost unreadable without context; the diagonal slash thins out and the four ticks muddle together. It might work at 32px+ only, which makes it a weak favicon. If you love the concept, we'd need to explore whether a simplified "3 ticks + slash" version could survive small.

If I had to pick one, it would be T3. The registration mark is the least inventive of the four and the one I'd bet holds up longest. It trades signature for conviction — it stops trying to be clever and just is a mark about precision, used for precision. The brand around it can do the specific work; the mark just has to be trustworthy at favicon size forever.

But T1 is right there, and if you want the brand to lean measurement-literal, T1 is the defensible choice. The framed pair of ticks is a mark that could only belong to this product.
