§ Brand Exploration · v1

Four directions for the Good Measure mark.

Each shown at three sizes — nav (14px), section header (48px), and poster (120px) — in both light and inverted. Read them first at nav size; that's where they'll live most of the time. The rest is how they scale.

Direction 01
Wordmark · no mark
Typography is the brand

Good Measure, set carefully.

No icon. No accent dot. The wordmark itself is the logo, set in DM Sans at a deliberate weight (500, not default bold) with precise tracking. This is the Milly Dent move — the restraint is the statement. What makes it opinionated rather than default is the tracking and the weight choice; everything else is DM Sans at rest.

What it's doing
Nothing, on purpose. The weight (500) and tight tracking are the only moves. It's the most defensible option — and the hardest to get wrong.
What it costs
No "thing that's only yours." No favicon icon. No mark to stamp on physical goods. Risk: reads as a startup that hasn't branded yet.
Favicon / app icon
A single letter (G or M) in DM Sans 700, or a large square with "GM" set small inside. Workable but generic.
Cascade
Every app surface just uses the wordmark. No complications, no decisions to re-make later. The ruler in the margin dies — nothing to echo.
Direction 02
Wordmark + ruler tick
The measure is literal

A single tick, standing in for a ruler.

A short vertical rule sits to the left of the wordmark — not a dot, not an icon. A mark, in the printmaker's sense. It reads as one tick of a ruler, or a measurement indicator, or simply as a pause before the word. At poster scale the tick becomes a full inch-mark with subdivisions; at nav scale it's a single hairline. The ruler in the landing page becomes the brand's native texture.

What it's doing
Making the name's double meaning literal — this is good measure as a unit of measurement. The ruler tick also echoes the landing page's margin ruler, so the system is coherent.
What it costs
At 14px the tick becomes very small — risks reading as a decorative pipe character. Needs careful stroke-width management at every size.
Favicon / app icon
Strong. The ruler mark alone, cropped to a square. Instantly recognizable and unique.
Cascade
Ruler in landing margin stays and is strengthened. Stamp / sticker / letterhead work is natural. You could literally print a ruler and ship it to beta users.
Direction 03
Wordmark + abstract mark
Precision, not literal

A circle divided.

A perfect circle, split by a thin horizontal line — suggesting a scale, a balance point, a half-mark. It's the "50%" indicator, the meridian, the split of a two-person household. Specific enough to mean something, abstract enough to not pin to any single reading. The geometry is the content: two halves, measured.

What it's doing
Reading as a unit ("half / whole / balance") without naming it. The circle is geometric and confident — the simplest mark that could be a logo.
What it costs
Risk of generic — a circle with a line is a shape many brands land on. Could read as a sunset, a cookie, a horizon, a half-moon. Needs the wordmark to disambiguate.
Favicon / app icon
Strong. The divided circle is iconic and unique at a glance. Would make a great iOS app icon (circle inside rounded square).
Cascade
The mark becomes a stamp language — the circle-with-line appears as a section break, a divider, a loading indicator. Versatile.
Direction 04
Stacked wordmark · no mark
The wordmark as a masthead

Good / Measure — stacked, ruled.

The two words stack with a horizontal rule between them, like a journal masthead. "Good" on top, "Measure" below, a thin line holding them together. Still typography only — no custom mark — but the treatment is unmistakable. It scales beautifully and it's instantly typeset-feeling. At nav size it horizontals out to the standard "Good Measure." The stacked form lives on covers, posters, and the app's opening surface.

What it's doing
Treating the wordmark like a masthead. The rule between "Good" and "Measure" is the unit-of-measurement echo — a ruler mark, buried inside the name. Functions everywhere, breathes on covers.
What it costs
Two versions to maintain (horizontal for nav, stacked for display). Slight risk of reading as "Good / Measure" as two separate words if someone skim-reads.
Favicon / app icon
Weak. You'd need a fallback mark for small sizes — probably a single letter or a horizontal rule as a placeholder.
Cascade
Strong for editorial moments — hero of the landing page, app opening screen, print work. Weaker in high-frequency small-size contexts (nav, favicons, iOS shortcuts).

§ My read
If you asked me to pick

Direction 02 (ruler tick) is the strongest. Here's the argument.

It does the most work per unit of visual noise. The tick at nav size is nearly invisible — but it's there, and it's specific to you. At poster size it becomes a real ruler, which turns the name's double meaning into a literal object. The favicon problem solves itself (the ruler mark alone, cropped). It cascades into the landing page's margin ruler you already like, into stamped physical goods you might make, into a visual language for the app where rulers and tick marks become a texture.

Direction 01 (wordmark only) is the safest. If you want to make a decision that's impossible to be wrong, this is it. You lose the signature, but you never regret the mark you chose.

Direction 03 (divided circle) is beautiful but most likely to feel generic in a year — there are only so many circles with lines through them. I'd pass.

Direction 04 (stacked masthead) is the most editorial and the most dangerous. It's gorgeous on a cover and awkward in a nav. It's really a second wordmark, not a logo — and maintaining two wordmarks is more work than it's worth unless you commit to it.
